**Q41.** 🟡 Prove that $A - B = A \cap B'$.

> [!success] Proof
> Let $x \in A - B$
>
> $\Rightarrow x \in A$ and $x \notin B$
>
> $\Rightarrow x \in A$ and $x \in B'$
>
> $\Rightarrow x \in A \cap B'$
>
> So $A - B \subseteq A \cap B'$.
>
> Conversely, let $x \in A \cap B'$
>
> $\Rightarrow x \in A$ and $x \in B'$
>
> $\Rightarrow x \in A$ and $x \notin B$
>
> $\Rightarrow x \in A - B$
>
> So $A \cap B' \subseteq A - B$.
>
> Therefore $A - B = A \cap B'$. $\blacksquare$

**Q44.** 🟡 If $A \subseteq B$, prove that $A \cup B = B$.

> [!success] Proof
> **Need to show:** $A \cup B = B$, i.e., $A \cup B \subseteq B$ and $B \subseteq A \cup B$.
>
> **Part 1:** Let $x \in A \cup B$ $\Rightarrow$ $x \in A$ or $x \in B$.
> If $x \in A$: since $A \subseteq B$, $x \in B$.
> If $x \in B$: $x \in B$ directly.
> So $A \cup B \subseteq B$.
>
> **Part 2:** $B \subseteq A \cup B$ always (by definition of union).
>
> Therefore $A \cup B = B$. $\blacksquare$

**Q45.** 🟢 In a survey of 400 students, 100 were listed as taking apple juice, 150 taking orange juice, and 75 taking both. Find: (i) students taking at least one juice, (ii) students taking only apple juice, (iii) students taking only orange juice, (iv) students taking neither.

> **(iii) Only orange:**
> $$n(O) - n(A \cap O) = 150 - 75 = \boxed{75}$$
>
> **(iv) Neither:**
> $$n(U) - n(A \cup O) = 400 - 175 = \boxed{225}$$

**Q47.** 🟡 Prove De Morgan's Second Law: $(A \cap B)' = A' \cup B'$.

> [!success] Proof
> **Part 1:** Show $(A \cap B)' \subseteq A' \cup B'$
>
> Let $x \in (A \cap B)'$
> $\Rightarrow x \notin A \cap B$
> $\Rightarrow$ NOT ($x \in A$ and $x \in B$)
> $\Rightarrow x \notin A$ or $x \notin B$
> $\Rightarrow x \in A'$ or $x \in B'$
> $\Rightarrow x \in A' \cup B'$
>
> So $(A \cap B)' \subseteq A' \cup B'$.
>
> **Part 2:** Show $A' \cup B' \subseteq (A \cap B)'$
>
> Let $x \in A' \cup B'$
> $\Rightarrow x \in A'$ or $x \in B'$
> $\Rightarrow x \notin A$ or $x \notin B$
> $\Rightarrow$ NOT ($x \in A$ and $x \in B$)
> $\Rightarrow x \notin A \cap B$
> $\Rightarrow x \in (A \cap B)'$
>
> So $A' \cup B' \subseteq (A \cap B)'$.
>
> Therefore $(A \cap B)' = A' \cup B'$. $\blacksquare$

**Q48.** 🔴 Prove that for any sets $A$, $B$, $C$:
$$A \cup (B \cap C) = (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$$

> [!success] Proof
> **LHS to RHS:**
>
> Let $x \in A \cup (B \cap C)$
> $\Rightarrow x \in A$ or ($x \in B$ and $x \in C$)
>
> **Case 1:** $x \in A$ $\Rightarrow$ $x \in A \cup B$ and $x \in A \cup C$ $\Rightarrow$ $x \in (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$
>
> **Case 2:** $x \in B \cap C$ $\Rightarrow$ $x \in B$ and $x \in C$ $\Rightarrow$ $x \in A \cup B$ and $x \in A \cup C$ $\Rightarrow$ $x \in (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$
>
> So $A \cup (B \cap C) \subseteq (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$.
>
> **RHS to LHS:**
>
> Let $x \in (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$
> $\Rightarrow$ ($x \in A$ or $x \in B$) AND ($x \in A$ or $x \in C$)
>
> If $x \in A$: $x \in A \cup (B \cap C)$ directly.
>
> If $x \notin A$: then from the first condition $x \in B$, and from the second $x \in C$. So $x \in B \cap C$, hence $x \in A \cup (B \cap C)$.
>
> So $(A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C) \subseteq A \cup (B \cap C)$.
>
> Therefore $A \cup (B \cap C) = (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$. $\blacksquare$
