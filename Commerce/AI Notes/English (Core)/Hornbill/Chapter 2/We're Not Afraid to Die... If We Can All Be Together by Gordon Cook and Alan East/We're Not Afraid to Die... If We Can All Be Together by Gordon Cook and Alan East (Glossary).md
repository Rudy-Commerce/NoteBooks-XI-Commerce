# 📖 CHAPTER 3 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY
> **We're Not Afraid to Die... if We Can All Be Together** | All terms alphabetically arranged with precise definitions, textual examples, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

> [!info] **Adventure Narrative** *(Board/CUET)*
> A genre of prose in which the narrator undergoes a dangerous journey or trial, typically told in the first person. *We're Not Afraid to Die...* is a **true adventure narrative** — the events actually happened to the author's family.
>
> *Contrast with: Fiction (invented events), Autobiography (full life account)*

---

> [!note] **Anchor (as symbol)**
> In the story, the island of Île Amsterdam functions as the crew's psychological anchor — the single fixed point of hope in an otherwise featureless and dangerous ocean. Navigating toward it gives the narrator purpose and the crew a goal.

---

## B

> [!note] **Bow (of a ship)**
> The front end of a boat or ship. When a massive wave strikes the bow, the force is transferred along the entire length of the vessel — one reason the storm's impact on Wavewalker was so catastrophic.

---

## C

> [!important] **Character Sketch** *(Board/CUET)*
> A description of a character's personality, traits, and values supported by textual evidence. Commonly asked for the narrator (father), Sue, or Jonathan.
>
> *Key traits to include for the narrator: courageous, skilled, self-sacrificing, devoted father, calm under pressure.*
>
> *Key traits for Sue: selfless, emotionally strong, physically resilient, loving.*

---

> [!note] **Collision Mat**
> A large piece of material (often canvas or heavy fabric) used to cover a hole or crack in a ship's hull from the outside, reducing water ingress. The narrator uses one alongside wooden plugs to seal the Wavewalker's cracked hull.
>
> *Exam tip: This detail shows the narrator's practical seamanship — he knows exactly what to do even when in pain.*

---

> [!note] **Contrast (Literary Device)** *(Board/CUET)*
> The juxtaposition of opposing qualities, characters, or situations to highlight differences.
>
> | Contrast Pair | Effect |
> |:---:|:---|
> | Children's calm vs adults' physical pain | Makes the children the moral centre of the story |
> | Sue's injuries vs her comforting note | Creates the story's most powerful irony |
> | The vast, indifferent ocean vs the tiny, determined family | Heightens the sense of human courage |

---

> [!note] **Courage**
> One of the story's two central themes (alongside love). Courage in this story takes multiple forms:
>
> | Type of Courage | Character | Example |
> |:---:|:---:|:---|
> | Physical | Narrator | Works through broken ribs, missing teeth, severe injuries |
> | Physical | Mary | Steers for hours through storm conditions |
> | Emotional | Sue (7) | Hides cracked ribs; writes comfort note to father |
> | Emotional | Jonathan (6) | Never cries; speaks the title's defining words |
> | Professional | Larry and Herb | Pump water in exhausting continuous shifts |

---

## D

> [!important] **Dead Reckoning** *(Board/NEET — technical term)*
> A method of calculating one's current position using a previously known position, then advancing that position using known or estimated speeds, headings, and time elapsed — without GPS or visual landmarks.
>
> *In the story:* The narrator uses dead reckoning to navigate toward Île Amsterdam across vast open ocean with damaged instruments. His calculation proves accurate to within **10 miles** — an extraordinary feat of seamanship.
>
> > ⚠️ Exam Tip: Dead reckoning is NOT the same as guessing. It is a precise mathematical method. The narrator's accuracy demonstrates years of skill and preparation.

---

> [!note] **Determination**
> The quality of continuing to pursue a goal despite obstacles. Every character in the story demonstrates determination — but the narrator's determination is the most physically tested: he continues to work, navigate, and lead despite severe injuries, because he has no other choice if his family is to survive.

---

## E

> [!note] **Ellipsis (...)**
> A punctuation device using three dots to indicate a pause, a trailing thought, or omitted words. The title uses an ellipsis: *"We're Not Afraid to Die... if We Can All Be Together."*
>
> *Significance:* The pause after "die" forces the reader to absorb the word before the conditional clause transforms it. Death is acknowledged directly, not softened — and then conditioned on togetherness.

---

> [!note] **Enjambment**
> In narrative prose, the equivalent device is the **run-on sentence** or paragraph that maintains tension by moving quickly from one event to the next without resolution. The narrator's account during the storm uses rapid, breathless sentences to create this effect.

---

## F

> [!important] **First-Person Narration** *(Board/CUET)*
> A narrative in which the story is told by a character within the story using "I." In this text, the narrator is the father himself.
>
> *Effect:* Creates immediacy and intimacy — the reader experiences the danger as the narrator experiences it. It also means the narrator's knowledge is limited to what he can see and feel — which creates suspense (he does not know Sue's injuries until later).

---

> [!note] **Foreshadowing**
> Hints or clues planted early in a text about events that will occur later.
>
> *Example:* The darkening skies and heavy swells described before the storm foreshadow the catastrophe to come. The narrator's mention of careful preparation ("16 years") foreshadows that this skill will matter when tested.

---

## G

> [!note] **Gordon Cook**
> The real person whose voyage forms the basis of this story. He is both the story's narrator and its central character. Co-author Alan East helped adapt the account for publication.

---

## H

> [!important] **Hyperbole** *(Board/CUET)*
> Deliberate exaggeration for effect.
>
> *Example:* *"Waves the height of a five-storey building"* — while the waves really were approximately 15 metres, the comparison to a "five-storey building" makes the scale viscerally understandable to a reader who has never seen such a wave.
>
> *Effect:* Conveys the superhuman scale of the storm; makes the family's survival feel all the more extraordinary.

---

## I

> [!important] **Île Amsterdam** *(Board — location and symbolism)*
> A small French island in the southern Indian Ocean. It is the destination the narrator navigates toward using dead reckoning after the storm.
>
> | Literal Role | Symbolic Role |
> |:---:|:---|
> | The nearest safe harbour in the region | Hope — the one point of safety in a vast ocean |
> | The goal of the navigation | Purpose — it gives the narrator something to aim for |
> | The site of their rescue and recovery | Survival — the physical embodiment of their triumph |
>
> Finding it within 10 miles of the calculated position is the story's climactic triumph of skill over catastrophe.

---

> [!important] **Imagery** *(Board/CUET)*
> Language that creates vivid sensory pictures. The storm sequence is rich in imagery:
>
> | Image | Sense | Effect |
> |:---:|:---:|:---|
> | *"Walls of water"* | Visual | The wave as a solid, vertical mass — a wall, not just water |
> | *"Monstrous waves"* | Visual | The sea personified as a creature |
> | *"Wooden plugs driven into cracks"* | Visual + Tactile | Practical heroism made concrete and immediate |
> | *"Bailing out water in the darkness"* | Visual + Physical | The exhaustion and desperation of the survival effort |

---

> [!important] **Irony** *(Board/CUET)*
> A contrast between expectation and reality.
>
> Two key ironies:
>
> | Irony | Expectation | Reality |
> |:---:|:---:|:---|
> | Sue's note | A severely injured child would need comfort | She gives it instead — to her injured father |
> | Jonathan's words | A six-year-old would be the most afraid | He provides the story's philosophy of fearlessness |

---

## J

> [!important] **Jonathan** *(Board — character and title connection)*
> The narrator's six-year-old son. He is injured (bumped his head in the storm) but never cries. He asks cheerful, practical questions about the journey. His words — *"We're not afraid to die, Daddy, if we can all be together"* — become the story's title.
>
> His significance: innocence speaking the deepest philosophical truth. A six-year-old's acceptance of death is conditional only on his family being together — this makes love, not survival, the story's ultimate value.

---

## L

> [!note] **Larry Vigil and Herb Seigler**
> Two professional sailors picked up in Cape Town to supplement the family crew. During the storm and its aftermath, they pump water almost continuously — exhausting physical labour that keeps the boat from sinking.
>
> *Exam tip: They represent professional skill and quiet, unglamorous heroism — they do not say remarkable things; they simply do what is needed.*

---

> [!note] **Leadership**
> The narrator demonstrates leadership throughout the crisis. Key leadership acts:
> - Concealing his injuries to maintain crew morale
> - Organising pumping shifts — turning panic into routine
> - Making practical repairs under impossible conditions
> - Navigating without full instruments — trusting his preparation
>
> *His leadership is defined by what he does NOT show (fear, pain, doubt) as much as what he does.*

---

## M

> [!important] **Mary** *(Board — character significance)*
> The narrator's wife. She is often underestimated in exam answers. Her heroism is **physical and active**:
> - She steers the boat (takes the helm) through the storm for hours — an exhausting, dangerous task requiring strength and skill
> - She pumps water continuously alongside the professional crew
> - She does not panic or break down at any point
>
> > ⚠️ Do NOT describe Mary as merely "supportive" or "the mother." Her role is actively heroic.

---

## N

> [!note] **Narrative Non-Fiction**
> Writing that tells a true story using literary techniques (character, plot, tension, imagery). *We're Not Afraid to Die...* is narrative non-fiction — the events are real, but the account uses all the tools of storytelling.

---

## O

> [!note] **Ocean (Southern Indian Ocean)**
> The setting of the storm and survival sections. The southern Indian Ocean is one of the world's most remote and dangerous stretches of water — vast, stormy, and far from shipping lanes. This geographic fact intensifies the isolation of the family's situation.

---

## P

> [!note] **Plymouth**
> The English port city from which the narrator's family departs in July 1976. Plymouth is historically significant — it was the departure point of many famous voyages, including those of Captain James Cook (whose circumnavigation the narrator seeks to replicate).

---

> [!note] **Preparation**
> The narrator spent **16 years** preparing for this voyage — building experience, studying navigation, purchasing and outfitting Wavewalker. This preparation is not background detail — it is what makes survival possible. Dead reckoning, hull repair, seamanship — all are fruits of preparation.

---

## R

> [!note] **Resilience**
> The ability to recover from adversity. Every character demonstrates resilience, but the children — Jonathan and Sue — demonstrate it most vividly. Both are injured, both are in genuine danger, and both respond with cheerfulness, practicality, and love.

---

## S

> [!important] **Sue (Suzanne)** *(Board — most emotionally significant character)*
> The narrator's seven-year-old daughter. Her significance:
>
> | Detail | Significance |
> |:---:|:---|
> | Injuries: black eye, swollen cheek, cracked ribs, torn skin | Severe — worse than the narrator knows during the crisis |
> | She hides all injuries from her father | Selfless — she protects his morale above her own pain |
> | Writes the note | *"Daddy, you are the best daddy in the world and the best captain"* |
> | Draws pictures to cheer the narrator | Active love — she gives comfort, doesn't just withhold complaint |
>
> > ⚠️ Sue's note is the story's most moving moment — a physically suffering child prioritising her parent's emotional state. This is the story's central irony and its greatest emotional impact.

---

> [!note] **Seamanship**
> The skill of operating and navigating a vessel at sea. The narrator's seamanship is repeatedly tested — repairing the hull, cutting away damaged rigging, rationing supplies, and navigating to Île Amsterdam using only dead reckoning. His 16 years of preparation translate into this seamanship.

---

> [!important] **Suspense** *(Board/CUET)*
> The tension created by uncertainty about the outcome. The narrator creates suspense through:
> - Introducing new crises before resolving previous ones
> - Withholding information (his own injuries, Sue's full injuries)
> - The navigation sequence — will they find the island?
> - First-person narration that keeps the reader inside the crisis

---

## T

> [!important] **Theme** *(Board/CUET)*
>
> | Theme | Key Evidence |
> |:---:|:---|
> | Courage | Narrator works through broken ribs; Mary steers through storm |
> | Family love | Jonathan's words; Sue's note |
> | Human spirit in adversity | No character gives up at any point |
> | Skill and preparation | Dead reckoning; hull repairs; 16 years of readiness |
> | Hope | Île Amsterdam as the goal sustaining the crew |

---

> [!note] **Togetherness**
> The value at the heart of the story's title. Jonathan conditions his acceptance of death on the family being together. This makes togetherness — not survival — the story's ultimate value. The story asks: what is survival worth without the people you love?

---

## U

> [!important] **Understatement** *(Board/CUET — key device)*
> Describing something in deliberately restrained, less emphatic terms than the situation warrants.
>
> *Example:* *"Things didn't look good"* — said at the point when the boat is flooding, the mast is broken, the narrator has broken ribs, and the family is alone in a vast, stormy ocean.
>
> *Effect:* The restraint creates tension precisely by not matching the language to the scale of the disaster. It also characterises the narrator — a man of professional composure, not theatrical emotion.
>
> A second form: the narrator **never directly tells** his family how badly he is injured. This concealment is itself a form of understatement — managing information to protect morale.

---

## V

> [!note] **Voyage**
> An extended journey by sea. The voyage in this story is an attempt to circumnavigate the world — replicating the historical voyages of Captain James Cook. The ambition of the voyage gives the storm its dramatic weight: they had sailed so far; they had so far to go.

---

## W

> [!important] **Wavewalker** *(Board — vessel name)*
> The name of the narrator's boat. A 23-metre (approximately 76-foot) wooden-hulled vessel, professionally built for the circumnavigation. The name is ironic — a vessel named for walking through waves is nearly destroyed by them.
>
> *Exam tip: Students sometimes write "the ship" or "the vessel." The correct name is Wavewalker.*
