# PART B — SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS (3–4 Marks, 80–100 Words)

---

**Q36.** 🟢 What steps did the narrator take to repair and save the Wavewalker after the storm?

> [!success] Model Answer
> After the storm cracked the hull, the narrator worked methodically through severe personal injuries. He made **wooden plugs** to hammer into the largest cracks in the hull, slowing the inflow of water. He also rigged a **collision mat** — a piece of heavy material placed over the hull's exterior to reduce flooding. He organised the crew into continuous **pumping shifts**, ensuring the water level never rose fatally. He cut away damaged **mast and rigging** that posed further danger. Each action was taken calmly and precisely, despite broken ribs and other injuries — the direct product of 16 years of preparation.

---

**Q37.** 🟢 What are Sue's injuries, and why is her note to the narrator so significant?

> [!success] Model Answer
> During the storm, Sue suffers a black eye, a severely swollen and bruised cheek, cracked ribs, and a large piece of skin torn from her back. She conceals all of this from her father throughout the crisis. She then writes him a note: *"Dear Daddy, I love you. You are the best daddy in the world and the best captain."* She also draws pictures of their adventure to cheer him. The note is the story's most moving moment because a severely injured seven-year-old is prioritising her father's emotional state above her own physical suffering. It is the purest example of selfless love in the story.

---

**Q38.** 🟢 How does Jonathan contribute to the story's emotional impact?

> [!success] Model Answer
> Jonathan is six years old during the crisis. Despite a bump on his head, he never cries and asks cheerful, practical questions — his equanimity itself sustains morale. His most important contribution is his response when the narrator asks if the children are afraid: *"We're not afraid to die, Daddy, if we can all be together."* These words become the story's title. They represent the story's central theme — that love makes even death acceptable, and that togetherness is more important than survival. A child's words carry the story's philosophy, making innocence the vehicle for its deepest truth.

---

**Q39.** 🟡 Describe the narrator's navigation to Île Amsterdam. Why is this achievement significant?

> [!success] Model Answer
> After the storm, the narrator navigates toward the tiny French island of Île Amsterdam in the vast southern Indian Ocean using **dead reckoning** — calculating position from known speed, heading, and time elapsed, without GPS or functioning instruments. Days later, a lookout spots the island. The narrator's calculation has been accurate to within **10 miles** — an extraordinary feat. The achievement is significant for two reasons: it saves the family's lives, and it demonstrates that the narrator's 16 years of preparation were not background detail but the direct cause of their survival. Skill and discipline triumphed over catastrophe.

---

**Q40.** 🔴 *"Love is stronger than the fear of death."* Discuss with reference to the story.

> [!success] Model Answer
> This statement is the story's philosophical core, embodied most directly in Jonathan's words — *"We're not afraid to die, Daddy, if we can all be together."* He does not say they are unafraid of death; he says the condition for accepting death is togetherness. Family love makes even death acceptable. Sue's note carries the same love in a different form — a physically suffering child suppressing her pain to comfort her father. The narrator's concealment of his own injuries is also love: he absorbs suffering to protect his family's hope. The storm tests whether the family will survive; love is what makes them want to.

# PART C — LONG ANSWER QUESTIONS (6 Marks, 120–150 Words)

---

**Q41.** 🟢 Draw detailed character sketches of Jonathan and Sue. What do they contribute to the story's emotional impact?

> [!success] Model Answer — 6 Marks
> Jonathan and Sue are the story's emotional heart — two children whose courage, in very different ways, transforms an adventure story into a meditation on love.
>
> Jonathan is six years old. He is injured — a bump on the head — but never cries. He asks cheerful, practical questions throughout the crisis, becoming a source of quiet morale for the adults. His defining moment comes when the narrator asks if the children are afraid. His answer — *"We're not afraid to die, Daddy, if we can all be together"* — gives the story its title and its philosophy. A six-year-old's acceptance of death, conditioned entirely on family togetherness, makes love the story's ultimate value.
>
> Sue is seven years old. Her injuries are far more severe: a black eye, a swollen and bruised cheek, cracked ribs, and torn skin. She hides every one of these injuries from her father throughout the crisis. When she finally communicates with him, it is through a note: *"Dear Daddy, I love you. You are the best daddy in the world and the best captain."* She also draws pictures of the adventure to cheer him. A severely injured child prioritising her parent's emotional state above her own physical pain is the story's most moving act. Sue's courage is not the courage of ignorance — she knows exactly how bad things are. It is the courage of love.
>
> Together, the children invert the expected dynamic: the smallest, most vulnerable family members become the moral and emotional anchor of the family's survival.

---

**Q42.** 🟡 *"The story is as much about skill and preparation as it is about courage."* Discuss.

> [!success] Model Answer — 6 Marks
> The most obvious reading of *We're Not Afraid to Die...* is a story of courage against impossible odds — and this reading is correct. But it is incomplete. The story equally argues that courage without preparation leads to death.
>
> The narrator spent **16 years** preparing for this voyage. He trained in seamanship, studied navigation, and chose his vessel — Wavewalker — carefully. When the crisis came, this preparation manifested in concrete actions: he knew how to make wooden plugs for a cracked hull; he knew how to rig a collision mat; he knew dead reckoning well enough to navigate a tiny island across a vast ocean to within 10 miles. None of these acts were improvised; all were the product of years of deliberate preparation.
>
> The professional crewmen, Larry and Herb, represent the same principle — they are not family members motivated by love; they are skilled professionals who do what needs doing. Their contribution — continuous, exhausting pumping — is less dramatic than Jonathan's words or Sue's note, but equally necessary.
>
> The story does not suggest that courage alone is enough. It suggests that when courage and skill work together — when a family that loves each other also has the training to survive — human beings can endure almost anything. The two themes are inseparable: you need the courage to keep going and the skill to know what to do.
