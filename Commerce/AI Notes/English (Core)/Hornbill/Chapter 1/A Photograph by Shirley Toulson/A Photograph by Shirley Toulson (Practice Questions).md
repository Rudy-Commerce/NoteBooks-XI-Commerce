# PART B — SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS (3–4 Marks, 80–100 Words)

> [!info] Attempt Strategy
> Begin with a direct statement. Provide 2–3 textual points. End with a brief conclusion. Write in complete sentences.

---

**Q36.** 🟢 What does the word "cardboard" tell us about the photograph and its role in the poem?

> [!success] Model Answer
> By calling the photograph "cardboard," the poet emphasises its material nature — it is an ordinary, perishable object made of printed card. This seemingly plain description carries a quiet irony: such a humble, fragile object holds an irreplaceable moment. The word also subtly reminds us that memory's vessels are themselves temporary — the photograph that preserves the past is itself subject to decay. It is one of the poem's understated ways of exploring impermanence: even the object that holds memory is mortal.

---

**Q37.** 🟢 What is the significance of "her laughter was important"?

> [!success] Model Answer
> This short, emphatic sentence is the most emotionally loaded line in Stanza 2. The word "important" is a deliberate understatement — far from meaning merely "significant," it carries the full weight of an irreplaceable, precious shared experience. The mother's laughter was the living connection between the photograph and the past; it animated the memory. Now that the mother is dead, that laughter is permanently lost. The brevity and simplicity of the line enacts the irreversibility of the loss — there is nothing more to say.

---

**Q38.** 🟢 Explain the significance of the sea in the poem.

> [!success] Model Answer
> The sea appears across all three stanzas and is the poem's most complex symbol. In Stanza 1, it is the physical setting — a backdrop to the beach photograph, suggesting permanence. In Stanza 2, it becomes a memory anchor — the mother talks of "the beach from their childhood." In Stanza 3, the poet strips the sea of its apparent permanence: "both the people and the sea / are there for their time, but for their time only." By declaring the sea also temporary, the poet deepens the poem's central argument — nothing, not even the eternal-seeming natural world, is exempt from impermanence.

---

**Q39.** 🟡 What is the effect of the parallel between the mother's age in the photograph and the years she has been dead?

> [!success] Model Answer
> The mother is approximately twelve years old in the beach photograph. By the poem's present, she has been dead for nearly as many years. This parallel — youth measured against death — is the poem's most powerful structural device. It binds all three time frames together, suggesting that time measures itself in cycles: the same span that gave her childhood now marks her absence. It also implies that the years of her death have now consumed the very age she was when the photograph was taken — as if time has erased even the image.

---

**Q40.** 🔴 *"Silence silences"* — explain the device used and its significance as the final line of the poem.

> [!success] Model Answer
> The final line uses **tautology** — the word "silence" appears as both noun (subject) and verb (predicate). Rather than being redundant, this self-referential construction is deeply intentional: the sentence enacts what it describes. In using language to say that language fails, the sentence performs its own collapse. The poet has been silenced by grief so profound that no articulate expression is adequate. The line also ends the poem — the poem itself is silenced here. As the poem's final statement, it argues that the deepest grief exists beyond words; that the only honest response to such loss is to stop speaking.

# PART C — LONG ANSWER QUESTIONS (6 Marks, 120–150 Words)

---

**Q41.** 🟢 The poem *A Photograph* deals with three distinct time frames. Explain each, and show how the poem moves from joy to silence.

> [!success] Model Answer — 6 Marks
> *A Photograph* is organised around three moments in time, each one a further remove from the original joy. In the first time frame, the photograph itself — the earliest past — shows the poet's mother at approximately twelve years old, paddling on a beach with her two girl cousins, Betty and Dolly. They are mid-laugh, mid-action, preserved forever in the image. This stanza is the poem's brightest point: youth, sea, and joy are all present.
>
> In the second time frame — some twenty to thirty years later — the mother, now an adult, shows the photograph to the poet, who is herself about twelve. The mother laughs and talks of the past, of the cousins, of the beach. But Betty has already died. Time is already consuming the people in the photograph. The laughter in this stanza is warm but shadowed.
>
> In the third time frame — the poem's present — the mother herself has been dead for nearly twelve years. Only the photograph and the poet's grief remain. Even the sea, which seemed to be the poem's one permanent element, is declared "there for its time only." The final line — "silence silences" — enacts the poem's conclusion: joy gives way to laughter; laughter gives way to silence; and silence is the only language left for such loss.

---

**Q42.** 🟡 *"A Photograph is a meditation on time, not just on grief."* Discuss.

> [!success] Model Answer — 6 Marks
> To call *A Photograph* solely a poem about grief would be to understate its philosophical ambition. The poem is, at its core, a meditation on time — and grief is what time leaves behind when it takes the people we love.
>
> The poem's structure embodies this meditation. It moves across three time frames: the photograph (the earliest past), the moment of shared laughter (the intermediate past), and the silent present. Each transition is a loss — not sudden, but accumulated, as time strips away one source of joy after another.
>
> The poem also refuses the consolation of permanence. The sea — the one element that appeared to resist time — is declared in the final stanza to also exist "for its time only." This is the poem's most philosophically significant claim: nothing is exempt from time. Not people, not nature, not laughter, not even the photograph that holds them.
>
> The number twelve appears three times — the mother's age in the photograph, the poet's age when shown it, and the approximate years since her mother's death. Time measures itself in cycles, and those cycles have now consumed the very youth they once contained. The poem argues that time is not merely the setting for grief — it is grief's architect.
