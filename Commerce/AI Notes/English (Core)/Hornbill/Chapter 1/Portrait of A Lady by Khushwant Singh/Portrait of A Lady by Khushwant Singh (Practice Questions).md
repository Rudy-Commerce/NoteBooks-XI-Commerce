# PART B — SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS (3–4 Marks, 80–100 Words)

> [!info] Attempt Strategy
> Begin with a direct statement. Provide 2–3 supporting points from the text. End with a brief conclusion. Write in full sentences — not bullet points.

---

**Q36.** 🟢 How did the grandmother and narrator's relationship change when the family moved to the city?

> [!success] Model Answer
> In the village, their bond was intimate and daily — she walked him to school, helped him prepare, and they spent all day together at the temple school. In the city, this closeness broke down. He travelled by bus to an English-medium school alone; she could not understand his lessons in science and English, and was troubled by music being taught. With no shared space left, she retreated to prayer, the spinning wheel, and the sparrows. The narrator himself notes the "thread of their relationship" had become "thin and strained." Affection remained; companionship was lost.

---

**Q37.** 🟢 Why was the grandmother disturbed by music being taught at the city school?

> [!success] Model Answer
> The grandmother came from a deeply traditional and religious background. She associated music with dancing girls and what she called "the vulgar and the common" — company she considered morally unrefined and unsuitable for a decent young man. The village temple school had taught prayer and scripture, which she valued and understood. The city school's curriculum — science, English, and now music — seemed spiritually empty and morally questionable to her. Her disapproval was not mere stubbornness; it was rooted in her belief that education must be anchored in faith and moral character.

---

**Q38.** 🟢 Describe the sparrows' behaviour at the grandmother's death. What does it signify?

> [!success] Model Answer
> When the grandmother's body was laid out, thousands of sparrows gathered silently on the verandah around her. They did not chirp. When the narrator's mother laid out bread for them, they did not eat. When the body was carried away, they flew off quietly. This scene is the story's most symbolic moment. The sparrows had been her daily companions — she fed them every afternoon in the city. Their silent mourning suggests that genuine love extends beyond the human world. Their wordless grief is presented as deeper and more authentic than articulate human sorrow.

---

**Q39.** 🟡 Why does Khushwant Singh call the portrait of his grandmother "impersonal"? What is the significance of the title?

> [!success] Model Answer
> The painted portrait hanging in the house captured only the grandmother's physical appearance — a stern, old woman. It told the viewer nothing of her warmth, her daily prayers, her love for sparrows, or the quiet dignity with which she lived and died. Singh calls it "impersonal" because paint cannot capture a person's soul. This is the central irony of the title: the story *The Portrait of a Lady* is itself the real portrait — detailed, intimate, and full of the living woman. The painting is the lesser likeness; the written prose is the true one.

---

**Q40.** 🟡 What does the grandmother's drumming and singing on the narrator's return reveal about her character?

> [!success] Model Answer
> The grandmother singing old folk songs and playing the drum on the narrator's return from abroad was completely out of character — she was always quiet, composed, and prayerful. This one burst of exuberance reveals how deeply she had missed him and how profoundly joyful his return made her. It also makes her death the following day deeply poignant. She had saved her greatest expression of joy for this moment, as though instinctively completing her emotional life before its end. It shows that beneath her composed exterior lived a woman of profound feeling.

---

**Q41.** 🔴 *"Love can survive separation even when closeness cannot."* Discuss with reference to the story.

> [!success] Model Answer
> This statement captures the story's emotional core. The grandmother's love for the narrator never diminishes across three phases of growing physical and intellectual distance. In the village, they are inseparable. In the city, her world and his grow incompatible — but she never reproaches him. Abroad for five years, she accepts the separation without bitterness. On his return, she celebrates with song. On her deathbed, she refuses to talk but keeps praying — including, presumably, for him. The love is never withdrawn; it simply finds fewer channels of expression as modernity builds walls between their worlds.

# PART C — LONG ANSWER QUESTIONS (6 Marks, 120–150 Words)

> [!info] Structure for 6-Mark Answers
> Introduction (1–2 sentences restating the question) + 3–4 developed points with textual evidence + Conclusion (1–2 sentences). Write in connected paragraphs, not bullet points.

---

**Q42.** 🟢 Draw a detailed character sketch of the grandmother as portrayed in *The Portrait of a Lady*.

> [!success] Model Answer — 6 Marks
> The grandmother in Khushwant Singh's sketch is one of the most memorable figures in Class XI literature — not because she is dramatic or extraordinary, but because she is profoundly, quietly human. Physically, she is old, short, slightly bent, and white-haired, with lips perpetually moving in silent prayer and fingers always on the rosary. Yet Singh insists she was beautiful — "like the winter landscape in the mountains, beautiful and stark." This is not the beauty of youth but the beauty of completeness.
>
> She is deeply religious, but her faith is not performance — it is the architecture of her daily life. When her role in the narrator's life diminishes, she deepens her prayer; God fills the space the world has vacated. She is selfless — she never demands attention, never reproaches the narrator for his growing distance, and never makes her feelings a burden to others.
>
> Most movingly, she is compassionate toward all living things. Her daily feeding of dogs and sparrows is an extension of the same love she gives to people — the sparrows' silent mourning at her death is the story's tribute to this quality. She is a portrait of dignity, faith, selfless love, and quiet courage: a woman left behind by the modern world but never diminished by it.

---

**Q43.** 🟡 *"The Portrait of a Lady is not merely a story about an old woman but a meditation on the price of progress."* Discuss.

> [!success] Model Answer — 6 Marks
> Khushwant Singh's autobiographical sketch operates on two levels simultaneously. On the surface, it is a gentle, affectionate portrait of his grandmother — her appearance, her habits, her devotion. At a deeper level, it is an honest examination of what modern progress costs in terms of human connection.
>
> The narrator's journey from village to city to abroad is also a journey away from his grandmother — not through ill will, but because the world she inhabited and the world he entered were growing irreconcilably apart. She could not follow him into English grammar, Western science, or music lessons. He could not stay behind with her in prayer and the spinning wheel.
>
> Each step of his education, while expanding his world, narrowed hers. The village temple school was the last space they truly shared — where education and faith were one, and she could walk beside him. The city school and the journey abroad removed her from his life in stages, each one irreversible.
>
> What makes the story tender rather than bitter is that the love never disappears — it simply finds fewer channels of expression. The grandmother never reproaches, never protests. She feeds sparrows, prays silently, and once, at his return, sings with a drum. Singh's genius lies in showing that modernity's cost is not dramatic — it is quiet, cumulative, and almost imperceptible. Yet it is real.
