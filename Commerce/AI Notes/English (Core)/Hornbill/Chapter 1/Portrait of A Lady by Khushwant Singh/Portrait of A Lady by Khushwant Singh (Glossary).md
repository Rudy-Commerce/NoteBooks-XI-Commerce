# 📖 CHAPTER 1 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY
> **The Portrait of a Lady** | All terms alphabetically arranged with precise literary definitions, textual examples, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

> [!info] **Autobiographical Sketch** *(Board/CUET)*
> A short prose piece in which the **author writes about their own life** — a specific memory, relationship, or phase of life — in first person. It is neither pure autobiography (which covers a whole life) nor pure fiction.
>
> *This story is an autobiographical sketch because Khushwant Singh writes from his own experience, using "I" as narrator, about his real grandmother.*
>
> *Contrast with: Short story (fictional), Autobiography (full life account)*

---

> [!note] **Austere**
> Severely simple; without ornamentation or luxury. Used in the story to describe the grandmother's appearance.
>
> *"Like the winter landscape in the mountains, beautiful and stark"* — "stark" carries the meaning of austere.
>
> *Exam use: Describe the grandmother's beauty as austere rather than ornate.*

---

## B

> [!note] **Beads (Rosary Beads)**
> Small balls threaded on a string, used to count prayers in religious practice. The grandmother is **always** described as telling (counting) her beads.
>
> *Significance: The rosary is her constant companion — it is both a religious object and a symbol of her identity.*

---

## C

> [!important] **Character Sketch** *(Board/CUET — very important)*
> A written description of a character's personality, traits, values, and behaviour — supported by evidence from the text. The grandmother is the most commonly asked character for a sketch in this chapter.
>
> *Key traits to include: religious, selfless, dignified, compassionate, accepting, traditional.*

---

> [!note] **Compassion**
> Deep sympathy and care for others, expressed through action. The grandmother's compassion is shown not through words but through repeated acts — feeding dogs, feeding sparrows, praying for others.
>
> *Board tip: Use "compassion" rather than "kindness" for a more precise and impressive answer.*

---

> [!note] **Contrast (Literary Device)**
> The juxtaposition of two opposing ideas, characters, or settings to highlight differences. Used extensively in this story:
>
> | Contrast Pair | Purpose |
> |:---:|:---|
> | Village school vs city school | Shows the divide between tradition and modernity |
> | Painted portrait vs written story | Shows the irony of the title |
> | Narrator's modern world vs grandmother's traditional world | Central tension of the story |

---

## D

> [!note] **Desolation / Seclusion**
> A state of isolation or emptiness. In the city phase, the grandmother voluntarily retreats into seclusion — she spends her days in her room, spinning and praying.
>
> *"She accepted her seclusion with resignation"* — key quoted line. "Resignation" here means peaceful acceptance, not unhappiness.

---

> [!note] **Dignity**
> The quality of being worthy and composed, especially under difficult circumstances. The grandmother never weeps, never reproaches, never makes her grief a burden to others. Her death itself is dignified — quiet, prayerful, on her own terms.

---

## E

> [!important] **Extract-Based Questions** *(Board/CUET — exam format)*
> Questions where a passage from the story is given and students answer MCQs or short questions based on it. Most commonly extracted passages:
>
> 1. The "winter landscape" simile — *appearance and beauty*
> 2. The grandmother's "seclusion with resignation" — *city phase*
> 3. The sparrows refusing bread — *final symbolic scene*
>
> *See Practice Questions file for model answers.*

---

> [!note] **Exaggeration / Hyperbole**
> A figure of speech that overstates for dramatic effect. The simile *"her face almost white as the white dressing-gown she wore"* functions as a kind of hyperbole — it exaggerates the whiteness to intensify the emotion of fear or shock.

---

## F

> [!note] **First-Person Narrator**
> A narrator who uses "I" and is a character within the story. In this sketch, the narrator is Khushwant Singh himself — making the narrative autobiographical.
>
> *Effect: Creates intimacy and authenticity; we trust the narrator's observations about the grandmother because they are personal memories, not invented details.*

---

> [!note] **Foreshadowing**
> A literary device where hints or clues are planted early in the text about events that will occur later.
>
> *Examples in the story:*
> - She senses the departure to abroad may be their last meeting
> - Her silence and prayer at the railway station foreshadows her death
> - The narrator says *"she had a premonition of her death"*

---

## G

> [!important] **Genre** *(Board/CUET)*
> The category or type of literary work. *The Portrait of a Lady* belongs to the genre of **autobiographical sketch** — a sub-genre of creative non-fiction.
>
> | Genre | Key Feature |
> |:---:|:---|
> | Short story | Fictional plot with invented characters |
> | Autobiographical sketch | Real events, author as narrator, specific memory |
> | Autobiography | Full life account in first person |
> | Biography | Life account written by someone else |

---

> [!note] **Grief (Wordless)**
> The story's most powerful moment of grief is expressed not by humans weeping but by **sparrows remaining silent**. This is the author's deliberate choice — wordless grief is presented as deeper than articulate grief.
>
> *"There was no chirping"* — most quoted line from the ending.

---

## H

> [!note] **Hornbill**
> The name of the Class XI NCERT English Core textbook in which *The Portrait of a Lady* appears as the first prose chapter. Named after the hornbill bird.

---

> [!note] **Humility**
> A quality of being modest and unpretentious. The grandmother never boasts, complains, or draws attention to her own sacrifice. Her humility is part of her dignity.

---

## I

> [!important] **Imagery** *(Board/CUET — frequently asked)*
> Language that appeals to the senses and creates a vivid mental picture. Key images in the story:
>
> | Image | Sense | Effect |
> |:---:|:---:|:---|
> | *"Winter landscape in the mountains"* | Visual | Austerity, timelessness, beauty |
> | *"Lips moving in silent prayer"* | Visual + Auditory | Inward, constant spirituality |
> | *"Rosary slipping from her fingers"* | Visual + Tactile | Life and prayer ending together |
> | *"Thousands of sparrows... no chirping"* | Visual + Auditory | Profound, wordless mourning |

---

> [!note] **Irony** *(Board/CUET — title analysis)*
> A contrast between appearance and reality, or between what is said and what is meant.
>
> *In this story:* The title "The Portrait of a Lady" refers to a painting that the narrator calls "impersonal." The true, living portrait is the **story itself** — rich, warm, and full of the real woman. The painting = lesser portrait. The prose = the real one.

---

## K

> [!note] **Khushwant Singh (1915–2014)**
> Indian author, journalist, lawyer, and public intellectual. Known for satirical wit, candour, and secular humanism. Major works: *Train to Pakistan*, *The History of the Sikhs*, *Delhi*.
>
> *The Portrait of a Lady* is one of his most tender and widely taught pieces.

## M

> [!important] **Modernity vs Tradition** *(Board/CUET — central theme)*
> The most tested theme of the story. Modernity (English education, science, music, city life) progressively isolates the grandmother from the narrator's world.
>
> *Each phase of the narrator's education = more distance from her world:*
> 1. Village temple school → religion and learning unified → she belongs
> 2. City English school → science, music → she is excluded
> 3. Abroad → five years on another continent → she is left entirely behind

---

## N

> [!note] **Narrator**
> The voice telling the story. In this sketch, the narrator is Khushwant Singh himself. The first-person narration creates intimacy and ensures the reader trusts the emotional truth of the observations.

---

> [!note] **Nostalgia**
> A sentimental longing for the past. The entire sketch is written in a nostalgic tone — the narrator looks back on his grandmother with affection, tenderness, and a gentle sadness for time passed and distance grown.

---

## P

> [!note] **Polysyndeton**
> A figure of speech in which conjunctions (usually "and") are used repeatedly in quick succession.
>
> *Example from Steinbeck referenced in Chapter 6 (Creative Writing):* "they cackled **and** fought **and** shouted **and** threatened"
>
> *Effect:* Creates a sense of rapid, overlapping action. Mirrors the pace and energy of the scene.

---

> [!important] **Portrait** *(Board/CUET — title meaning)*
> A representation of a person — can be painted, photographed, or written.
>
> *In this story:* The "portrait" has a double meaning — the literal painting that is called impersonal, and the literary portrait that is the story itself.

---

> [!note] **Premonition**
> A strong feeling that something (usually bad) is about to happen. The narrator says the grandmother *"had a premonition of her death"* — she sensed her end was near and began to accept it spiritually.

---

> [!note] **Prose**
> Ordinary written language, without the regular rhythmic structure of poetry. *The Portrait of a Lady* is prose — specifically, lyrical and imagistic prose that occasionally approaches poetry in its imagery and rhythm.

---

## R

> [!important] **Resignation** *(Board — frequently misread word)*
> In the story, the word "resignation" means **peaceful acceptance** — not unhappiness or giving up in defeat.
>
> *"She accepted her seclusion with resignation"* means she accepted it calmly, without protest, with spiritual equanimity.
>
> ⚠️ Common exam mistake: Students write "she was sad" or "she gave up." The correct reading is that she accepted — with grace and faith.

---

> [!important] **Rosary** *(Board/CUET — symbol)*
> A string of beads used in prayer. The grandmother's rosary is her most constant companion throughout the story.
>
> | Moment | Significance of Rosary |
> |:---:|:---|
> | Always in her hands | Prayer is her constant state, not a periodic act |
> | At the farewell | She prays silently for the narrator |
> | At her death | Rosary slips from her fingers — prayer and life end together |

---

## S

> [!important] **Seclusion** *(Board)*
> A state of isolation or withdrawal from others. In the city phase, the grandmother accepts seclusion — she retreats to her room, spinning wheel, and prayer when she can no longer participate in the narrator's modern school life.

---

> [!important] **Simile** *(Board/CUET — most tested device)*
> A figure of speech comparing two different things using "like" or "as."
>
> | Simile in text | What is compared | Effect |
> |:---:|:---:|:---|
> | *"Like the winter landscape in the mountains, beautiful and stark"* | Grandmother to a mountain winter scene | Timeless, austere, dignified beauty |
> | *"Face almost white as the white dressing-gown she wore"* | Her face to a white garment | Exaggerates shock; intensifies emotion |

---

> [!important] **Symbolism** *(Board/CUET — most tested device alongside simile)*
> When an object, person, or event represents something beyond its literal meaning.
>
> | Symbol | Literal | Symbolic Meaning |
> |:---:|:---:|:---|
> | Rosary | Prayer beads | Faith, identity, lifeline to God |
> | Sparrows | Birds she fed | Devotion, companionship, silent grief |
> | Spinning wheel | A domestic tool | Tradition, patience, old India |
> | Drum | Musical instrument | The one burst of joy; poignant farewell to life |
> | Painted portrait | A painting | The lesser, impersonal representation of the woman |

---

## T

> [!important] **Theme** *(Board/CUET)*
> The central idea or underlying message of a literary work.
>
> | Theme | Key Sentence for Exams |
> |:---:|:---|
> | Modernity vs Tradition | Modern education creates distance; tradition is left behind |
> | Love without words | Love is expressed through acts, not declarations |
> | Passage of time | Time changes relationships even when love remains |
> | Faith and devotion | For the grandmother, prayer is not a ritual but a way of being |
> | Compassion | Her love extends to all living creatures, not just humans |

---

> [!note] **Tone**
> The author's attitude toward the subject, conveyed through word choice and style. The tone of this story is **nostalgic**, **tender**, **reflective**, and occasionally **elegiac** (mourning-like).

---

> [!note] **Tradition**
> Practices, beliefs, and customs passed down through generations. The grandmother represents tradition — faith-based education, music-free simplicity, daily prayer, feeding animals. The city and English school represent its opposite.

---

## V

> [!note] **Village School (Temple School)**
> In the story, the village school is attached to the local temple. The priest teaches the alphabet and prayers simultaneously. This unified model of education — where faith and learning are one — is something the grandmother can participate in fully. The city school, in contrast, is entirely secular and alien to her.

---

## W

> [!warning] **Wordless Communication** *(HOT — Higher Order Thinking)*
> Much of the grandmother's most powerful communication is wordless:
> - She prays silently at departure — says more than words
> - She sings and drums on his return — joy beyond speech
> - The sparrows sit silent at her death — grief beyond language
>
> The story consistently argues that the deepest emotion transcends language.
